# Memory: Chandragiri Escape 2026

## Project Snapshot
- Project type: Informational event microsite
- Project status: New build
- Core subject: Office outing to Chandragiri Hills Resort
- Event window: March 26, 2026 to March 28, 2026
- Primary goal: Present the full outing plan in one clear, attractive, mobile-friendly destination so attendees can quickly understand the itinerary, cable car timing constraints, activity options, pricing, payment notes, food details, and operational warnings.

## INTERNAL STRATEGY

### The Vision
Create a polished event-information website that feels welcoming, organized, and trustworthy without reading like a corporate portal. The experience should make attendees feel prepared before they arrive. It should reduce back-and-forth questions by putting the most time-sensitive information first, especially cable car operating-hour warnings and arrival timing expectations.

The site should work as a practical trip companion:
- Give attendees the full itinerary at a glance.
- Surface operational warnings early and repeatedly.
- Make optional paid activities easy to compare.
- Clarify what is included, what is extra, and how payments work.
- Present snack and menu information in a friendly, appetizing way.
- Feel like a memorable team getaway rather than a dry memo.

### Audience
Primary audience:
- Employees attending the office outing
- Internal coordinators sharing the event page with teams
- Late checkers who need the key facts in under a minute

Audience needs:
- Fast confirmation of date, venue, and schedule
- Clear notice about cable car cut-off times
- Simple overview of paid vs included activities
- Confidence about meal/snack arrangements
- Easy mobile reading during travel

### Persona And Tone
Brand personality:
- Warm
- Organized
- Scenic
- Calm under logistics
- Slightly celebratory

Voice characteristics:
- First-person plural
- Friendly and confident
- Clear about rules and timing
- Never stiff, legalistic, or overly promotional
- Crisp sentences for notices and schedules
- More vivid, sensory language for destination and food sections

Design identity:
- Mountain-resort editorial style
- Fresh daylight palette inspired by hill skies, forest greens, cable car steel, and warm wood
- Strong hierarchy with notice banners, timeline cards, pricing chips, and itinerary anchors
- Mobile-first reading flow with sticky quick-nav to sections like Itinerary, Cable Car Notice, Activities, Pricing, Food, Payments, FAQs

Recommended visual direction:
- Colors: pine green, mist white, slate, warm sandstone, sunrise orange for alerts
- Typography: expressive serif for headings paired with clean sans for body text
- Backgrounds: soft landscape gradients or topographic texture, not flat corporate white
- Motion: subtle reveal on itinerary items and notice cards, no excessive animation

### Information Architecture Priorities
Order content for maximum usefulness:
1. Hero with event name, dates, venue, and one-line summary
2. High-visibility cable car warning banner
3. Full itinerary / day-by-day timeline
4. Activity details with optional pricing
5. Payment notes and deadlines
6. Snack and menu information
7. Travel or preparation reminders
8. FAQ / contact block

### Content Requirements
The build must include dedicated coverage for:
- Full event itinerary
- Cable car timing notices
- Activity details
- Pricing for optional activities
- Payment notes
- Snack/menu information
- Warning section about cable car operating hours from March 26, 2026 to March 28, 2026

Important content behavior:
- Treat cable car hours as operationally critical information, not a footnote.
- Repeat timing warnings in the hero support text, the notice block, and near the itinerary where relevant.
- Use tables or cards for optional activity pricing.
- Separate included items from optional paid add-ons.
- If exact activity/vendor details are not yet confirmed, keep the structure ready and label placeholders clearly for later fill.

### Historical Context
This is a fresh project. No prior site, copy system, or asset library has been provided yet.

### Comparative Research Directives
No reference sites were supplied, so research should begin with real-world source gathering before visual synthesis.

Architect research tasks:
- Visit the official Chandragiri Hills or Chandragiri Cable Car web presence and extract real operational details that affect visitor planning.
- Verify whether cable car operating hours differ by weekday, weekend, holiday, maintenance notice, or weather condition.
- Confirm whether resort, hilltop, or activity timings differ from cable car boarding hours.
- Check whether food outlets, restaurants, or snack counters publish menu categories, opening windows, or package options.
- Identify the actual names of on-site attractions and optional paid activities that guests can book or join.
- Gather any publicly listed pricing for optional activities, tickets, entry fees, or bundled experiences if relevant to visitors.
- Review maps, transport notes, parking details, and arrival recommendations if they influence the itinerary.
- Check official social channels or recent announcements only for current operational notices that materially affect planning.

Research extraction rules:
- Pull factual details only: timings, names, fees, activity labels, operational cautions, and access notes.
- Avoid writing about source credibility inside UI copy.
- Do not invent activity prices, menu items, or hours.
- If exact March 26-28, 2026 operating hours are unavailable, note the latest confirmed schedule internally and design the site so those values can be updated quickly.
- Preserve a distinction between confirmed facts and organizer-provided custom itinerary details.

Competitive inspiration research:
- Review a small set of event microsites, retreat pages, resort itinerary pages, and travel-day landing pages.
- Extract layout patterns that improve clarity: sticky agenda nav, alert bars, expandable itinerary cards, quick facts rail, pricing matrix, FAQ accordion.
- Do not imitate generic wedding or tourism-site clichés unless they genuinely improve this use case.

### UX And Content Strategy Notes
- The warning section should look operationally serious and impossible to miss.
- Use timeline design to make the itinerary feel easy, not dense.
- Keep each section scannable with compact labels, icons, and summary rows.
- Include a quick facts strip near the top: dates, venue, reporting time, cable car note, meal coverage, payment status.
- Add anchors for fast jumps on mobile.
- Include a final reminder block for what attendees should carry or note before departure.

### Suggested Section Structure
1. Hero
2. Key facts strip
3. Cable car operating notice
4. Event itinerary
5. Activities and add-ons
6. Pricing and payment notes
7. Snacks and menu
8. Preparation checklist
9. FAQ / contact

### Marketing Copy Strategy
Copy should sound like the organizers are speaking directly to attendees. It should be practical first, atmospheric second.

Messaging pillars:
- Clarity: dates, times, venue, and action items are unmistakable
- Ease: attendees should know exactly what to expect
- Excitement: the outing feels scenic and special
- Preparedness: guests understand the cable car timing constraint early

Copy style rules:
- Use first-person plural throughout
- Prefer active voice
- Keep warning language concise and unmistakable
- Keep scenic copy grounded in real trip details
- Avoid hype-heavy tourism language
- Avoid third-person descriptions of the event

### Cable Car Warning Section
**Section title:**
Cable Car Timing Notice

**Primary copy:**
We need everyone to pay close attention to cable car operating hours during March 26-28, 2026. Our arrival, activity flow, and return timing depend on the cable car schedule, so late reporting can affect the day’s plan.

**Supporting copy:**
We’ll use this section to share the confirmed operating window, last boarding guidance, and any timing buffer we expect everyone to follow.

**Short alert variants:**
- We should arrive early enough to avoid missing the cable car window.
- We may need extra buffer time if operations are affected by crowd flow or weather.
- We should treat the final boarding guidance as a hard cutoff.
